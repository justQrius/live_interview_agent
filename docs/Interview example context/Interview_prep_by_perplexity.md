SECTION 7: COMMUNICATIONS COMPLIANCE & REGULATORY LANDSCAPE
Current Regulatory Priorities (2025-2026)
Financial regulators are intensifying compliance scrutiny and enforcement, creating urgency for compliance technology investment. The SEC's Division of Examinations and FINRA jointly outlined their 2025 examination priorities, emphasizing heightened focus on compliance enforcement, third-party risks, and technology-driven challenges. Key regulatory focus areas include:
​

Anti-Money Laundering (AML), Fraud, and Sanctions Compliance: FINRA noted deficiencies in customer identification programs (CIP), customer due diligence (CDD), and suspicious activity reporting (SAR). Firms have exhibited failures to classify relationships as customer relationships, leading to inadequate identity verification and insufficient suspicious activity identification. The June 2025 expansion of U.S. sanctions designations against cryptocurrency shadow banking networks required financial institutions to adjust systems within 72 hours—demonstrating the surgical precision and speed of modern regulatory response.
​

Artificial Intelligence Governance: Both SEC and FINRA emphasized AI governance and explainability. The SEC requires firms to have adequate AI policies and procedures for fraud prevention, back-office operations, AML, and trading functions. A 2025 GAO report highlighted that "insufficient explainability in AI models can inhibit a financial institution's understanding of a model's conceptual soundness and reliability, inhibit independent review and audit, and make compliance with laws and regulations more difficult". This emphasis on AI explainability creates competitive advantage for Smarsh's domain-trained, transparent AI models vs. black-box systems.
​

Third-Party Risk Management: FINRA published its first-ever section on third-party risk landscape in its 2025 Regulatory Oversight Report. Firms must establish and maintain supervisory systems and written procedures to ensure compliance when using third-party services, conduct due diligence, validate data protection controls in vendor contracts, and maintain comprehensive lists of third-party services used.
​

Regulation Best Interest (Reg BI) and Form CRS: Firms continue to exhibit deficiencies in conducting reasonable investigations before recommending securities, assessing suitability, and making excessive/unsuitable recommendations. Form CRS amendments and filing requirements have been delayed but continue to require accurate, timely disclosures.

Investment Adviser Compliance: Starting January 1, 2026, SEC-registered investment advisers (RIAs) and exempt reporting advisers (ERAs) are now classified as "financial institutions" under the Bank Secrecy Act, requiring full AML/CFT programs, SAR filing, and travel rule compliance. SECTION 9: MEDDIC/MEDDPICC SALES METHODOLOGY FOR SOLUTIONS ENGINEERS
Framework Overview and Application to Presales
MEDDIC (Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Identify Pain, Champion) and MEDDPICC (adds Paper Process and Competition) are widely adopted in enterprise software sales to rigorously qualify opportunities and navigate complex buying processes. For Solutions Engineers specifically, MEDDIC/MEDDPICC serves as a framework for consultative conversations that position the SE as a trusted advisor, not merely a product explainer.
​

Application of MEDDIC components for Smarsh presales:

Metrics (M): What quantified business outcomes does the prospect want to achieve? For compliance, this might include: % reduction in false positives; faster review time per communication; cost savings from eliminated legacy systems; regulatory examination findings; employee productivity improvements. SE role: Help prospect articulate metrics aligned to Smarsh's capabilities (e.g., "95% false positive reduction" is a key Smarsh capability).
​

Economic Buyer (E): Who has budget authority and business ownership for compliance? Typically CFO, Chief Compliance Officer (CCO), or Chief Risk Officer (CRO). SEs must understand economic buyer's priorities (cost reduction, risk mitigation, operational efficiency) and communicate ROI in business terms.
​

Decision Criteria (D): What criteria does the prospect use to evaluate solutions? Technical (system scalability, channel integration, API flexibility), operational (ease of use, deployment timeline, support quality), strategic (vendor viability, roadmap, partnership model), financial (pricing, licensing model, total cost of ownership). SE role: Understand decision criteria early and position Smarsh's strengths (e.g., "We support 100+ channels; competitor X supports 60").
​

Decision Process (DP): What are the formal and informal steps to reach a buying decision? Typically: (1) needs assessment/RFI; (2) vendor evaluation/demos; (3) POC; (4) competitive evaluation; (5) legal/security review; (6) CFO sign-off; (7) contract negotiation; (8) implementation planning. SE role: Map the process, identify potential bottlenecks (e.g., security review delays), and plan SE engagement at each stage.
​

Identify Pain (I): What underlying business problems drive the buying urgency? For compliance prospects, pain might include: recent regulatory examination findings, pending audits, remote work supervision gaps, legacy system technical debt, employee productivity loss from manual review. SE role: Probe beneath surface issues to understand root pain and quantify business impact.
​

Champion (C): Who internally will advocate for Smarsh after the SE leaves the room? Champions are typically power users who recognize the product's value and will evangelize internally. SE role: Identify champions early (often technical/compliance staff), involve them in POCs, arm them with data to persuade other stakeholders.
​

Paper Process (P) (MEDDPICC): What are the steps after the buying decision to reach a signed contract? Legal review, security assessment, vendor onboarding, compliance sign-offs, procurement negotiations. SE role: Proactively surface potential delays (e.g., security review requiring SOC 2, DPA, etc.) and propose solutions.
​

Competition (C) (MEDDICC/MEDDPICC): Who/what are we competing against? Incumbent vendors, DIY solutions, status quo, alternative budget priorities. SE role: Understand competitive positioning and differentiate based on Smarsh's strengths (e.g., petabyte-scale AI, cloud-native architecture).
​

Best Practices for Enterprise Sales Cycles
Effective SEs in complex, 6-12+ month sales cycles employ several best practices: (1) Establish credibility early: Deep product knowledge + business acumen = trusted advisor status; (2) Link every feature to customer pain/ROI: Avoid feature recitation; always connect Smarsh capabilities to customer metrics/success; (3) Build mutual action plans (MAPs): Jointly-created, customer-owned roadmaps with accountability on both sides accelerate progress; (4) Maintain cadence: Regular touchpoints (weekly calls, shared dashboards) keep deals moving; momentum loss in long cycles is dangerous; (5) Escalate appropriately: When technical discussions reach limit, bring in product, engineering, or executive leadership as appropriate; (6) Document next steps clearly: Every customer interaction should end with explicit next steps, owners, and dates.
