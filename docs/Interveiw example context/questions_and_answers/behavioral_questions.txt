## 1. A Technical Demo or PoC That Went Wrong

### Interviewer Intent (Why They Ask This)

Dominic is not testing whether things go wrong — he assumes they do. He is testing composure, judgment, and whether you protect trust when the stakes are high.

He is listening for:
- Do you stay calm under pressure?
- Do you default to honesty or spin?
- Can you recover value even when the tech fails?

### Core Story (Canonical Version)

"At Solsys, I was running a Proof of Concept for an enterprise customer where the demo environment failed shortly before a critical stakeholder session.

Rather than trying to patch things live or pretend the issue wasn't serious, I acknowledged the problem immediately and reframed the session. I pivoted away from a live demo and walked the stakeholders through the architecture, the data flows, and — most importantly — how the system would behave in failure scenarios.

We used real metrics from earlier test runs, screenshots from validated dashboards, and a whiteboard discussion focused on operational workflows rather than UI clicks.

After the session, I followed up with a clear remediation plan, timelines, and success criteria. We reran the live demo once stability was restored.

The customer actually told us afterward that the recovery increased their confidence — because in regulated environments, how a vendor handles failure matters more than a perfect demo."

### What You Are Quietly Demonstrating

Without saying it explicitly, this story shows:
- Executive presence under pressure
- Honesty without loss of authority
- Value-first thinking over feature theatrics
- Alignment with regulated, risk-aware customers

This is exactly how Smarsh customers think.

### 30-Second Version (When Time Is Tight)

"I've had demos fail. When that happens, I don't try to bluff or scramble. I acknowledge it, pivot to architecture and outcomes, and make sure the customer still understands how the system solves their risk.

In one PoC, that approach actually increased trust — because regulated customers care more about how you handle failure than polished demos."

### Dominic-Specific Follow-Up Pivot

If Dominic probes deeper with something like *"What would you do differently next time?"*:

"What I changed was tightening pre-demo validation and explicitly defining what 'success' meant before the PoC started — not just technically, but operationally. That reduced pressure on single demo moments and kept the deal focused on outcomes."

This signals maturity, not defensiveness.

### Red Flags You Are Actively Avoiding

- Blaming infrastructure or teammates
- Over-indexing on technical heroics
- Treating the demo as the product

You come across as the adult in the room — which is what enterprise presales leaders look for.

## 2. Handling a Sales Rep Who Promises a Feature That Doesn't Exist

### Interviewer Intent (Why Dominic Cares)

This is an integrity test disguised as a teamwork question.

Dominic is listening for whether you:
- Protect the company and customer long-term
- Can push back without burning internal relationships
- Understand that trust is a revenue multiplier in enterprise sales

### Core Principle (Say This Early)

"I never let a customer make a buying decision based on something that isn't real. Short-term wins built on false expectations always turn into long-term losses."

That single sentence anchors everything.

### Core Story (Canonical Version)

"I've been in situations where an Account Executive, under deal pressure, has overstated roadmap or implied functionality that didn't exist yet.

My approach is always to address it quickly and privately. I'll align with the AE first, clarify the gap, and explain the downstream risk — churn, escalations, or compliance exposure — if we let that expectation stand.

Then, with the customer, I reset expectations transparently and redirect the conversation toward what *is* possible today. That might mean showing an alternative workflow, a phased rollout, or clearly positioning something as roadmap with no contractual dependency.

In my experience, customers respect honesty — especially in regulated environments. Deals that survive that reset are far healthier and far more expandable later."

### What You're Demonstrating Subtly

- You protect revenue *after* the signature, not just before it
- You understand presales as risk management, not feature selling
- You can say "no" without being obstructive

This aligns strongly with Smarsh's compliance-first posture.

### 30-Second Version

"If Sales promises something that doesn't exist, I correct it — privately with the rep first, then transparently with the customer. I'd rather slow a deal than win it on false assumptions.

In regulated environments, integrity is a sales strategy, not a moral luxury."

### Dominic-Probe Ready Follow-Up

If Dominic asks *"What if the AE insists?"*:

"Then I escalate constructively. I'll bring in leadership or Product if needed, because protecting the customer's trust and the company's reputation matters more than any single deal."

That shows backbone without drama.

### Common Traps You Avoid

- Throwing Sales under the bus
- Publicly contradicting a rep in front of a customer
- Framing yourself as 'the blocker' instead of 'the risk owner'

## 3. A Challenging Customer Problem I Solved

### Interviewer Intent (What They're Really Testing)

This question is not about technical cleverness.

Dominic is listening for whether you:
- Can navigate ambiguity and conflicting stakeholder incentives
- Understand regulated customer psychology
- Solve *organizational* problems, not just technical ones

### Core Framing (Open With This)

"The hardest customer problems are rarely technical — they're alignment problems under risk."

That single line sets you apart immediately.

### Core Story (Canonical, Smarsh-Aligned Version)

"At Solsys, I worked with a large financial institution that was struggling with fragmented monitoring and security tooling.

Compliance cared about auditability and defensibility, Security cared about detection fidelity, IT cared about uptime and performance, and leadership cared about cost and regulatory exposure. Each group was 'right,' but they were optimizing for different outcomes.

My first step wasn't architecture — it was structured discovery. I ran separate sessions with each group to understand their non-negotiables, then mapped those concerns into a shared success definition.

From there, I designed a centralized Splunk-based architecture that consolidated logs across systems, but more importantly, standardized workflows: how alerts were triaged, how incidents were documented, and how audit evidence was produced.

During the PoC, we focused less on dashboards and more on proving operational outcomes — reduced investigation time, clearer audit trails, and fewer handoffs between teams.

The result was improved incident response times, stronger compliance posture, and internal alignment that hadn't existed before. The customer told us the biggest win wasn't the tooling — it was finally getting everyone to speak the same language."

### Why This Maps Cleanly to Smarsh

Without changing a word, this story demonstrates:
- Multi-stakeholder discovery in regulated environments
- Risk-driven architecture design
- Workflow validation over feature demos
- Trust-building under compliance pressure

It mirrors Smarsh's value proposition almost perfectly.

### 30-Second Version

"I worked with a regulated enterprise where compliance, security, and IT all had conflicting priorities.

Instead of jumping to architecture, I aligned stakeholders on success criteria first, then designed and proved workflows that met audit, security, and uptime needs simultaneously. The technical solution mattered — but alignment was what unlocked the win."

### If They Ask 'What Made It Hard?'

"What made it hard wasn't scale or tooling — it was that every decision had regulatory consequences. That forced discipline in discovery, validation, and communication."

This subtly shows maturity.

### Common Mistakes You Avoid

- Over-indexing on a single stakeholder
- Turning it into a Splunk feature pitch
- Claiming heroics instead of systems thinking

## 4. A Deal or PoC I Lost — and What I Learned

### Interviewer Intent (Why This Question Matters)

This is a maturity test.

Dominic is not looking for perfection. He is listening for:
- Ownership without self-flagellation
- Pattern recognition instead of excuses
- Evidence that you improved your operating model

Senior SEs don't just win deals — they evolve their process.

### Core Framing (Say This First)

"I've lost deals — and the ones that hurt the most are the ones that taught me the most about discovery discipline."

That sentence immediately disarms the question.

### Core Story (Canonical Version)

"Earlier in my presales career, I worked on a PoC where we proved the technology successfully — ingestion worked, dashboards looked good, and performance benchmarks were met — but we still lost the deal.

When we did the post-mortem, it became clear that while we had delivered a strong technical validation, we hadn't fully aligned on *decision criteria*. A key stakeholder cared less about technical depth and more about operational ownership and internal resourcing — something we surfaced too late.

That loss changed how I run PoCs.

Since then, I explicitly define success criteria before a PoC starts — not just what we'll prove technically, but who needs to sign off, what risks they're accountable for, and how the solution will be operationalized.

As a result, my later PoCs became shorter, more focused, and far more predictable — with fewer 'surprise' objections at the end."

### What You're Demonstrating

- You take responsibility without being defensive
- You understand that PoCs are decision tools, not science projects
- You improved your process in a concrete, repeatable way

This is exactly how experienced presales leaders think.

### 30-Second Version

"I lost a deal where the tech validated but the decision criteria weren't fully aligned.

That taught me to treat PoCs as decision frameworks, not demos — defining success, stakeholders, and risks upfront. Since then, I've had far fewer late-stage surprises."

### If Dominic Asks 'What Would You Do Differently Now?'

"I'd surface decision ownership earlier and test for deal risk before investing heavily in the PoC. If alignment isn't there, I'd pause rather than push forward blindly."

That signals confidence and discipline.

### Traps You Avoid

- Blaming pricing or competition
- Claiming the customer 'didn't get it'
- Treating the loss as an anomaly

## 5. Handling Multiple Priorities and Tight Deadlines

### Interviewer Intent (What They're Assessing)

This question isn't about personal productivity hacks.

Dominic is evaluating whether you can:
- Protect deal quality under pressure
- Prevent self-inflicted chaos in complex sales cycles
- Act like an owner of limited technical capacity

Senior SEs don't just work harder — they constrain work intelligently.

### Core Framing (Lead With This)

"When everything is urgent, prioritization becomes a risk management exercise."

That line reframes the question immediately.

### Core Story (Canonical Version)

"In enterprise presales, I'm often supporting multiple deals simultaneously — each with its own discovery calls, PoCs, internal reviews, and deadlines.

My first step is always to make priorities explicit. I align with Sales early on deal stage, decision timelines, and technical risk so I'm not treating every request as equally critical.

From there, I define clear scopes and success criteria for each engagement. If a PoC starts to expand beyond its original intent, I'll pause and reset expectations rather than absorb unbounded work.

I also communicate trade-offs transparently. If supporting one high-risk, late-stage deal means another needs lighter coverage, I make that visible early instead of failing silently.

That approach has allowed me to meet deadlines consistently without sacrificing quality — and more importantly, without burning trust with customers or Sales."

### What This Signals

- You manage time through structure, not heroics
- You understand opportunity cost in presales
- You're comfortable saying 'not now' when needed

This is exactly how leaders expect senior SEs to operate.

### 30-Second Version

"I prioritize by deal risk and impact, not noise. I align early on scope and success criteria, make trade-offs explicit, and reset expectations when needed. That keeps quality high even under tight deadlines."

### If Dominic Probes Further

If he asks *"What happens when Sales pushes back?"*:

"I bring the conversation back to outcomes — which deals are closest to decision, where technical risk is highest, and how we win responsibly. That usually reframes urgency into alignment."

### Traps You Avoid

- Claiming you 'just work longer hours'
- Treating burnout as a virtue
- Letting scope creep masquerade as responsiveness
